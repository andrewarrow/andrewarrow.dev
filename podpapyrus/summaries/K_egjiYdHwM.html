Ryan & Emily CLASH w/ Taylor Lorenz On School PHONE BANS

Phones in Schools: Why Blanket Bans Miss the Point

A smart, funny look at why confiscation is the wrong fix, and what actually helps kids learn healthy tech habits.

Every generation has its “thing” that adults swear is destroying kids. For my mom it was MTV. For me it was video games (Mortal Kombat was apparently going to turn me into a bloodthirsty ninja). For today’s teenagers, the villain is the smartphone.

And sure, I get it. Phones are distracting. They buzz, ding, and light up like little Vegas slot machines at exactly the wrong moment. Teachers don’t want a classroom full of kids silently scrolling TikTok instead of learning about mitochondria. Parents picture their child staring slack-jawed at an iPhone until their brain dissolves into a puddle of emojis. So the instinct is simple: take the phones away. Problem solved.

Except it isn’t.

School Is Not a Police State

The first problem with phone bans is enforcement. Teachers already juggle lesson plans, grading, teenagers with unlimited energy, and a caffeine habit that is one crisis away from collapse. Now we want them to moonlight as phone cops? Unsurprisingly, many don’t want to. And when they won’t, the responsibility lands on campus resource officers. Translation: police.

We’ve already seen what happens. In the 1990s schools panicked over beepers. Yes, beepers. The logic was that pagers were linked to drug dealing. The result was predictable. Kids were harassed, arrested, and left with criminal records over a buzzing gadget the size of a matchbox. If we replay that script with phones, we are just guaranteeing more unnecessary confrontations between students and police. Algebra class should not end with handcuffs.

The New Moral Panic

Every era has its moral panic, and once it’s over it looks ridiculous. Comic books were supposed to rot our brains. Rock music was supposed to deliver us straight to hell. Video games were supposed to turn us violent. Now the panic is about phones and teen depression.

But the evidence does not line up neatly with that fear. Researchers who have actually spent their careers on this, people like Candace Rogers and Alice Marwick, keep finding the same thing. Phones are not the root cause. Poverty is. Stressful home environments are. Growing up in a world that feels unstable and overwhelming is. Phones can magnify existing struggles, but they don’t create depression out of thin air. They are more like a thermometer that shows something is wrong, not the disease itself.

If we mistake the symptom for the cause, we never solve the real problems.

Inequity in Disguise

There is also the practical problem that rarely makes headlines. Not every kid walks into school with a shiny laptop. For a lot of students, the phone in their pocket is the only computer they own. It is their word processor, their calculator, their research tool. About fifteen percent of students are disabled, and many rely on phones for accessibility.

When schools ban phones outright, it is not the wealthiest kids who suffer. It is the ones already dealing with less. Unless lawmakers are willing to pass bans that come with massive funding for alternative devices and tech support, the result is just deeper inequity.

Learning Self-Control

Phones are not going anywhere. They are part of college, part of jobs, part of life. Pretending we can bubble-wrap kids until age eighteen and then unleash them into the adult world with zero practice is absurd. It is like refusing to teach children how to cross a street because cars are dangerous, then acting surprised when they step off the curb and get flattened.

What kids need is practice. They need to learn how to use technology with boundaries, how to put the screen down when it is not appropriate, how to separate tool from toy. If we want adults who can regulate themselves, we have to start while they are still students.

What “Screen Time” Really Means

The phrase “screen time” is tossed around like it is a diagnosis, but it is a useless measurement without context. Two hours spent scrolling endless videos that make you feel bad is not the same as two hours spent messaging friends, writing a story, or editing a short film.

Key Points

  • Schools are implementing phone bans, with students roughly 99-100% opposed to these policies
  • Taylor Loren opposes blanket laws enforced by police but supports teacher-led classroom policies like phone baskets
  • Historical precedent exists with 1990s beeper bans that led to hundreds of kids having police interactions and arrests
  • Phone bans may be enforced by campus resource officers, potentially leading to discriminatory harassment of students
  • These laws are connected to broader political efforts to restrict kids' internet access and implement content censorship
  • Taylor Loren was falsely accused of being paid by phone companies despite promoting child safety software for free
  • Research shows no clear correlation between phone use and increased anxiety/depression in children according to top researchers
  • Multiple factors contribute to youth mental health issues, including one in six kids growing up below the poverty line
  • Studies suggesting "brain drain" from nearby phones are considered dubious and often funded by biased sources
  • For many students, phones serve as their only word processor and learning aid, especially for disabled students (15% of kids)
  • Schools should teach self-regulation rather than complete bans to prepare students for adult technology use
  • Primary phone use among kids is texting and direct messaging with friends, not endless scrolling
  • Kids who get smartphones earlier actually show better mental health due to improved friend communication
  • Complete phone-free childhood may harm kids' ability to function competitively in modern society
  • The solution should focus on comprehensive data privacy reform rather than device bans
  • Schools simultaneously ban phones while partnering with surveillance companies using facial recognition on students
  • Future technology (Apple watches, smart glasses, wearables) will make phone-specific bans irrelevant
  • Parents should introduce phones gradually with restrictions and teach healthy internet habits while children are young
