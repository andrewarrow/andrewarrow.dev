Nuclear Historian Answers Nuclear War Questions | Tech Support | WIRED

Key Points

  • There are 9 countries with nuclear weapons: US, Russia, UK, China, France (officially allowed), plus Israel, India, Pakistan, and North Korea
  • The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (1968) designates only 5 countries as legally allowed to have nuclear weapons
  • Iran was not actively building nuclear weapons but was setting up conditions to potentially build them quickly if needed
  • The nuclear football is a suitcase allowing the US President to order nuclear strikes from anywhere outside the White House
  • The US has lost nuclear weapons through airplane crashes and submarine accidents during the Cold War, some permanently
  • Hiroshima and Nagasaki were bombed instead of military targets because the US had adopted city-targeting tactics and the bombs worked best when detonated high above cities
  • Having more countries with nuclear weapons creates debate about safety - more weapons could mean more restraint or more accidents
  • Uranium enrichment separates uranium-235 (bomb-grade) from uranium-238 through centrifuges, requiring 80-93% enrichment for weapons vs 3% for power plants
  • Einstein only wrote a letter to Roosevelt about atomic research but wasn't involved in the Manhattan Project due to age and political concerns
  • Hiroshima and Nagasaki are not significantly radioactive today because bombs were detonated high in the air, preventing major fallout contamination
  • Nuclear bomb effects include instant fatal radiation within 3/4 mile, severe burns from heat hotter than the sun, and devastating blast waves destroying buildings for miles
  • Fallout shelters are designed for 2 weeks maximum stay, not decades like in fiction, using mass (concrete/dirt) to block radiation
  • Chernobyl is visitable today with safety precautions, though long-term residence increases cancer risk by about 5%
  • Radiation poisoning occurs when ionizing particles kill significant numbers of cells or damage DNA to cause cancer
  • Nuclear weapons can theoretically be unlimited in power, but the largest ever tested was the Soviet Tsar Bomba at 50 megatons
  • Bigger nuclear weapons become impractically large and heavy while damage increases slowly, making multiple smaller warheads more effective
  • Duck and cover provided some protection in the narrow zone between instant death and complete safety from nuclear blasts
  • Nazi Germany never came close to building atomic bombs, focusing instead on nuclear reactor research and concluding bombs were unnecessary for winning WWII
