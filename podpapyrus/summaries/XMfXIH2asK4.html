Bryan Johnson's Religion For The Age Of Artificial Intelligence

Key Points

  • Brian Johnson transitioned from Mormon entrepreneur to biohacking pioneer, selling Braintree to PayPal and later founding Kernel (brain-computer interfaces)
  • Started Blueprint protocol in 2022 as "world's best rejuvenation athlete" - training 6 hours daily on anti-aging protocols with rigorous scientific measurement
  • Uses controversial methods like erection monitoring devices, finding that discussing sexual health gets people's attention about sleep and health more than traditional messaging
  • Evolved from being sensitive about media criticism to embracing negativity as an energy source and power tool for public engagement
  • Experimented with psychedelics (5-MeO-DMT from Sonoran toad) and stem cell treatments, calling the toad experience his most profound life moment, now tattooed on his body
  • Considers Blueprint a stepping stone to his larger "Don't Die" philosophy/movement, planning to step back from the supplement business to focus on this mission
  • Believes AI progression will create massive societal disruption requiring new ideological frameworks, positioning "Don't Die" as humanity's response to unprecedented technological change
  • Grew up in rural Mormon Utah community, married young, had children, then underwent complete life transformation at 34 after divorce and leaving the church
  • Views current moment as potentially the first generation that won't die, requiring abandonment of all previous philosophical frameworks based on mortality
  • Participates in exclusive influencer groups where major business deals, relationships, and world decisions actually happen behind the scenes
  • Went from Silicon Valley outsider unable to raise money for Kernel to being sought after by billionaires and celebrities at elite gatherings
  • Advocates extreme humility about predicting the future, comparing humans today to Homo erectus trying to understand modern civilization
  • Runs "Don't Die" dinners with thought experiments designed to help people grapple with existential questions about technology and mortality
  • Plans to scale "Don't Die" ideology to potentially one billion people by 2030, exploring various community-building formats from intimate gatherings to corporate capital allocation
  • Considers wealth and fame isolating but prefers fame over wealth, finding it provides access to the technological and intellectual communities he values most
  • Believes traditional institutions (capitalism, democracy, religion) are insufficient for handling coming AI-driven societal changes
  • Advocates for complete personal reinvention periodically, feeling compelled to "shed skin" and rebuild identity when phases feel complete
  • Sees "Don't Die" as practical philosophy focused on survival and adaptation rather than traditional religious or spiritual framework
