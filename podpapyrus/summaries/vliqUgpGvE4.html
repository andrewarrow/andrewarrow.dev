Why We’re Drawn to Death, Crime, & Danger - Coltan Scrivner

Summary

A psychology researcher explains morbid curiosity as humans' adaptive interest in learning about threats from a safe distance. He identifies four domains: violence, dangerous people's minds, bodily injuries, and supernatural threats. This curiosity evolved because learning about dangers without experiencing them firsthand provides survival advantages - like gazelles observing cheetahs or humans telling stories about predators. The research shows morbidly curious people were more psychologically resilient during COVID-19, suggesting the trait helps prepare for real threats. Gender differences exist: women prefer true crime (relevant to their typical threat patterns), while men gravitate toward war stories. Horror films work by pairing vulnerable protagonists with powerful antagonists, and zombie movies succeed because they tap all four morbid curiosity domains simultaneously. Contrary to popular belief, horror fans aren't less empathetic - they actually need empathy to engage with protagonists in danger.
