Marc Maron explains why he tried to get out of the Bruce Springsteen biopic

Key Points

  • Mark Maron has become "offer only" and says no to most acting opportunities due to lack of desperation or hunger
  • He has a pattern of initial resistance to projects, even good ones, often forgetting to read scripts for weeks
  • His manager understands this behavior and works within it, waiting for "cracks in the wall" to persuade him
  • Maron tried to back out of the Springsteen movie "Deliver Me from Nowhere" initially
  • He felt insecure about taking a small role after doing a lead in an indie film
  • Previous bad experience being cut from a Michael Keaton movie about post-9/11 lawyers affected his confidence
  • He directly texted director Scott Cooper questioning if there was enough for him to do in the role
  • Cooper's response that "it would be fun" made Maron reconsider and agree to do the project
  • Maron doesn't typically think of work projects as "fun" - even Comic-Con was just "something he had to do"
  • He played Chuck Plotkin, a real sound engineer who was pivotal to solving recording problems
  • The real Bruce Springsteen and Jon Landau were on set in "Video Village" watching filming
  • Springsteen told Maron "You're him. You're Chuck" after his first scene, validating his performance
  • Jeremy Strong (playing Jon Landau) stayed in character but could access Jeremy to have conversations
  • Strong suggested adding empty food containers to make the long recording session more realistic
  • Springsteen specified they would have eaten "roast chicken and sweet potatoes" during long sessions
  • A PA had to find specific food props, demonstrating the lengths taken for authenticity
  • The specific food details changed the reality of the scene for both the environment and Strong's character work
  • Maron learned to operate a recording board for his role as the sound engineer
