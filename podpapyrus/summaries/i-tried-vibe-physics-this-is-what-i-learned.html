I tried Vibe Physics. This is what I learned.

Key Points

  • AI models are now being used to develop new physics theories, but often produce work that is either new or correct, rarely both
  • The author tested four large language models (GPT-5, Claude Opus 4.1, Grok 4, and Gemini Pro) on solving a millennium problem
  • The Navier-Stokes equation describes fluids and gases, used in climate and weather models
  • The millennium problem asks whether Navier-Stokes equations develop singularities from regular initial conditions
  • The author believes quantum physics is ultimately needed to prevent singularities, which Navier-Stokes lacks
  • The proposed solution involves using Einstein field equations with a suitable stress-energy tensor
  • The approach would identify geometry with fluid and use Penrose's singularity theorem to prove blowup
  • Known links between Navier-Stokes and general relativity mostly only work near horizons, limiting usefulness
  • GPT-5 performed best, roughly understanding the idea with reasonable procedural steps
  • Google DeepThink (€140/month) was very slow and produced grant proposal-like text rather than solutions
  • Gemini models showed confusion about basic physics concepts and lacked confidence in problem-solving
  • Claude Opus 4.1 was fastest but produced verbose, low-quality responses with basic physics errors
  • Grok 4 understood the concept better and provided pseudo-Python code, though not practically useful
  • Current AI models excel at literature research and explaining existing work for brainstorming
  • Major AI weaknesses include conflating similar physical concepts (energy vs free energy, time reversibility vs time reversal invariance)
  • Models often switch notation or topics mid-conversation, creating confusion for non-experts
  • AI doesn't develop genuinely new ideas but assembles plausible-looking arguments that skip actual proofs
  • Current AI models are not as capable as good students and physicist jobs remain safe from AI replacement
