25 Facts About Slavery Not Taught In Schools

Key Points

  • Slave breeding was systematically practiced after the Atlantic slave trade was abolished, forcing enslaved people to reproduce to increase owners' wealth
  • Medical experimentation was conducted on enslaved individuals without consent or anesthesia, including gynecological surgeries by Dr. James Marion Sims
  • Savage dogs were specifically bred and trained to hunt down runaway slaves, often mauling captured escapees as punishment and deterrent
  • The Zong mass killing involved drowning 132 enslaved Africans in 1781, initially treated as property loss for insurance purposes
  • Enslaved people were branded like cattle using heated irons, acid, or gunpowder as marks of ownership and dehumanization
  • African rulers and traders actively participated in selling fellow Africans to European slave ships in exchange for weapons and goods
  • Free black individuals were kidnapped and sold back into slavery through the "Reverse Underground Railroad" system
  • Men around 25 years old were considered the most valuable slaves due to their perceived peak physical ability for labor
  • Slaveholders created an edited "Slave Bible" that removed passages about freedom while emphasizing stories that normalized slavery
  • Physicians invented fake mental disorders like "drapetomania" to pathologize slaves who attempted to escape
  • Black people were arrested for unemployment after the Civil War, exploiting a loophole in the 13th Amendment for convict leasing
  • Illegal slave trade continued after official abolition, with 1.65 million Africans transported across the Atlantic during the 19th century
  • Slave quarters were overcrowded, unsanitary one-room cabins with dirt floors that spread disease and psychological trauma
  • Slave codes legally treated enslaved people as property, controlling movement, education, and family life while maintaining racial hierarchy
  • The domestic slave trade displaced 1.2 million people after 1808, separating families and creating constant fear of being sold
  • The Underground Railroad helped 100,000 enslaved people escape to freedom between 1810-1850 through a network of safe houses
  • Enslaved individuals worked in both rural plantations and urban areas as skilled tradespeople, contributing to city economies while facing surveillance
  • Modern slavery affects an estimated 49.6 million people today through human trafficking, forced labor, and forced marriage hidden in supply chains
