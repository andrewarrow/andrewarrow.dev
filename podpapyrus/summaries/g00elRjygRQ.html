The Atlantic Slave Trade: What Schools Never Told You

Key Points

  • 10-12 million African slaves were forcibly transported from Africa to the Americas between 1500-1880 CE, with about 15% dying during the journey
  • The majority of enslaved Africans came from six primary regions: Senegambia, Sierra Leone, Gold Coast, Bight of Benin, Bight of Biafra, and West Central Africa
  • The Middle Passage was the brutal Atlantic crossing that could take 3-6 months, with mortality rates of 15-25% for enslaved people and 20% for sailors
  • Ship conditions were inhumane: men had 6x1.4 feet of space, women 5x1.4 feet, with only 24 ounces of water daily and cramped quarters below deck
  • The Triangular Trade connected Europe (providing capital and manufactured goods), Africa (providing enslaved labor), and the Americas (providing raw materials like sugar, tobacco, cotton)
  • Only 4% of transported Africans (about 400,000) arrived directly in North America; the majority went to the Caribbean and South America, with nearly 5 million sent to Brazil
  • King Charles V of Spain granted the first charter in 1518 to transport 4,000 slaves directly from Africa to Spanish-American colonies
  • Before enslavement, Africans had diverse occupations including farmers, merchants, soldiers, musicians, goldsmiths, and blacksmiths - they were stripped of their identities
  • The 1839 Amistad revolt led by Joseph Cinque resulted in a landmark Supreme Court case that ruled the Africans were kidnapping victims with the right to escape
  • African participation in the slave trade included kidnapping, selling criminals and prisoners of war, and forming alliances with Europeans through intermarriage (kasari)
  • Some African kingdoms like Dahomey and Gold Coast elites had knowledge of slavery conditions through diplomats and educated children sent to Europe and America
  • By the 1690s, 30,000 people were taken from Africa yearly, growing to 85,000 per year by the late 1700s, with over 80,000 leaving annually in the 1820s
  • Seven European nations were major slave trading powers: Portuguese, British, French, Spanish, Dutch, Americans, and Danish
  • Islamic traders also exported 10 million slaves from Africa between 650-1900 CE to Arabia, Yemen, Iran, India, and Iraq via different routes
  • The slave trade caused massive demographic impact: by 1820, four Africans for every European had crossed the Atlantic, and 4 out of 5 women crossing were African
  • Scholar Adam Jones characterized the deaths as genocide and "one of the worst holocausts in human history," while others debate whether deaths were intentional or incidental to profit
  • The slave trade disrupted African societies, changing legal codes and punishment systems, with many crimes becoming punishable by enslavement rather than traditional penalties
  • Despite consequences, former slave-trading nations (UK, Portugal, Spain, Netherlands, US) have resisted formal apologies, likely due to concerns about monetary reparations
