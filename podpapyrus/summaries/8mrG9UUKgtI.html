The Diabolical History Of The Barbary Slave Trade

Key Points

  • The U.S. Marine Corps hymn lines "from the halls of Montezuma to the shores of Tripoli" commemorate battles in the Mexican-American War (1846-48) and fights against Barbary pirates
  • Barbary pirates terrorized the Mediterranean Sea and North African coast for three centuries, with "Barbary" being the anglicized version of "Berber"
  • The Barbary Coast stretched from eastern Morocco to Tripoli, approximately 1,000 miles
  • Berber tribes were joined by Muslim and Jewish refugees fleeing Spain during the Reconquista under Ferdinand and Isabella
  • Human slavery was the most lucrative commodity for pirates, more valuable than spices, gold, or gems from trade routes
  • Many Barbary pirates were actually Europeans acting as mercenaries, not native to the region
  • Early pirate ships were galleys powered by enslaved rowers, confining raids to the Mediterranean basin initially
  • Between 1609-1616, English merchants lost 466 ships to Barbary pirates, demonstrating the massive scale of piracy
  • The 1631 raid on Baltimore, Ireland saw Barbary corsairs travel over 1,000 miles to capture dozens of slaves
  • Male slaves faced hard labor in mines, shipbuilding, or the horrific fate of being chained galley slaves
  • Galley slaves were chained to oars permanently, eating and relieving themselves at their stations, leading to infection and death
  • Women slaves were sorted by attractiveness - unattractive ones did manual labor, attractive ones were sent to harems
  • Professor Robert C. Davis calculated at least 1.5 million European slaves were taken by Barbary pirates over two centuries
  • The pirate crews were diverse, including Berbers, Arabs, Turks, Dutch, Greeks, and even some English captains like Jack Ward
  • Notable pirates included Sayyida al-Hurra, an Arab Muslim woman who became governor of a Moroccan district
  • The Ottoman Empire had nominal control over the Barbary Coast, allowing local autonomy as long as they recognized the Sultan
  • The United States fought two Barbary Wars (1815 being the second) to protect American trade and sailors from piracy
  • European nations began systematic warfare against Barbary pirates from the mid-1600s, leading to the practice's eventual decline by the late 19th century
