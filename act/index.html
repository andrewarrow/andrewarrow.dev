1. Is Autism Hereditary?

Question: is austism heridary?

Yes, autism is highly heritable, meaning that genetics play a significant role in its development.

Here's a breakdown:

  • Twin studies show that if one identical twin has autism, the other has a 60–90% chance of also being diagnosed.
  • In non-identical twins, the rate drops to around 10–30%, showing a strong genetic component but also suggesting environment plays a role.
  • Siblings of children with autism are at a much higher risk (about 10–20 times the average).
  • Hundreds of genes and gene variants have been associated with autism, though no single "autism gene" causes all cases.

But not purely genetic:

  • Environmental factors, especially during pregnancy (e.g., maternal illness, exposure to toxins, or complications during birth), can also increase risk.
  • In most cases, it's a complex interplay of multiple genes and environmental influences, not just one or the other.

In short: yes, it's hereditary, but it's not 100% genetic or 100% inherited—it's a mix.

2. Grandparent Genetics

Question: if both my parents were austisic is it likely both sets of my grandparents were?

If both of your parents are autistic, it doesn't necessarily mean that all four of your grandparents were autistic too—but it does increase the chances that some of them may have had autistic traits or carried genetic variants associated with autism.

Here's why:

1. Autism is polygenic

Autism is influenced by many genes, not just one. Each parent may have inherited some of those genes from their parents (your grandparents) even if the grandparents themselves:

  • Didn't meet the criteria for an autism diagnosis, or
  • Had milder, "subclinical" traits (sometimes called Broader Autism Phenotype, or BAP), like:
    • Strong focus on routines
    • Social awkwardness or difficulty with change
    • Intense interests

2. Expression can vary

Just because someone carries autism-related genes doesn't mean they'll show full symptoms. The same genes may:

  • Show up as full autism in one person
  • Manifest as quirky personality traits in another
  • Stay hidden or "silent" in others

3. Environmental factors matter

Even with a strong genetic predisposition, factors like prenatal environment, birth complications, or toxins may influence whether someone is actually autistic.

So to your question:

  • It's likely that at least some of your grandparents carried autism-linked traits or genes.
  • But it's not required that all of them were autistic for your parents to be.

If you're curious, you could look at their behaviors or life stories through a modern lens—sometimes it becomes clearer in hindsight.

3. Paternal vs Maternal Contributions

Question: is there any weight to paternal vs maternal?

Yes, there's some evidence suggesting differences in maternal vs. paternal genetic contribution to autism—but it's not a simple one-side-dominates story. Here's a breakdown:

🔹 Paternal Line (Father's Side)

  • Older fathers are associated with a higher risk of autism in children. This is likely due to new (de novo) mutations that can arise in sperm as men age.
  • These mutations aren't inherited from grandparents, but rather spontaneously occur in the father's genetic material before conception.
  • Fathers may also pass on "silent" variants, which only cause symptoms when combined with other variants from the mother.

🔹 Maternal Line (Mother's Side)

  • Mothers often carry autism-linked genes without being diagnosed, possibly due to what researchers call the "female protective effect":
    • Girls may require more genetic hits to show clinical symptoms.
    • This means a mother could carry risk genes but appear neurotypical or only mildly affected.
  • Maternal immune activation (like infections or autoimmune conditions during pregnancy) may also play a role in some cases.
  • Some studies suggest that maternal family history might be slightly more predictive than paternal, but results are mixed.

🔹 X-Linked Factors

  • Since autism is more common in males (about 4:1 ratio), and males inherit their X chromosome from their mothers, some autism risk genes may be X-linked.
  • However, most autism isn't purely X-linked—it's more complex than that.

🔹 The Reality:

  • Both sides contribute, but through different mechanisms.
  • It's not that one parent's side is "more important"—it's that they might contribute different types of genetic risk.
  • The combination of variants from both parents, plus environmental factors, determines the final outcome.

Bottom line: Both your mother's and father's genetics likely contributed to your autism, but potentially in different ways. Neither side is "to blame"—it's just how genetic inheritance works!

3. Likely Inherited Variants

In essence: Your grandfather → father → you represents a classic example of how autism genetics can be transmitted through families. The consistency across generations suggests strong genetic factors at play.

5. Coping Strategies for Autistic Individuals

Question: what are some good coping strategies for someone who's autistic?

Here are some evidence-based coping strategies that many autistic people find helpful:

🧠 Understanding Your Own Autism

  • Know your triggers: Identify what overwhelms you (noise, crowds, changes, certain textures, etc.)
  • Know your strengths: Autism often comes with advantages like pattern recognition, attention to detail, deep focus, and unique perspectives
  • Know your needs: What helps you regulate? (quiet time, stimming, routine, special interests, etc.)

🛠️ Building Your Toolkit

Sensory Regulation:

  • Noise-cancelling headphones or earplugs
  • Fidget tools (stress balls, fidget cubes, etc.)
  • Comfortable clothing (seamless socks, soft fabrics)
  • Sunglasses for light sensitivity
  • Weighted blankets for calming pressure

Social Support:

  • Scripts for common social situations
  • Safe people you can be yourself around
  • Clear communication about your needs with family/friends
  • Autistic communities (online or local) where you're understood

Structure & Planning:

  • Visual schedules or calendars
  • Routine building around things that work
  • Preparation strategies for changes or new situations
  • Break tasks down into smaller, manageable steps

🎯 Specific Strategies

For Overwhelm:

  • Create a "quiet space" at home where you can retreat
  • Practice the "5-4-3-2-1" grounding technique: 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste
  • Use "scripts" like "I need a moment" or "I'm feeling overwhelmed"

For Social Situations:

  • Have an exit strategy ready
  • Take breaks (step outside, go to the bathroom, etc.)
  • Find the one person you connect with rather than trying to work the whole room
  • Be selective about which social events are worth your energy

For Executive Function:

  • Use timers for tasks (Pomodoro technique)
  • Body doubling (working alongside someone else)
  • External accountability (apps, friends, etc.)
  • Energy management rather than time management

💪 Leveraging Your Strengths

  • Lean into your special interests—they're often sources of joy and expertise
  • Use your attention to detail in work or hobbies
  • Trust your pattern recognition—you often see things others miss
  • Value your direct communication style—it's refreshing to many people

Most importantly: Coping isn't about "fixing" yourself or becoming neurotypical. It's about understanding how your brain works and creating an environment where you can thrive as you are.

6. Managing Creative Cycles and Burnout

Question: I get into these creative cycles where I'm super productive and then I crash and get depressed. any tips for managing that?

Oh, this is incredibly common for autistic people! You're describing what's often called the "spiky profile" of autism—intense capabilities followed by crashes. Here's how to manage it:

🔄 Understanding the Cycle

This isn't a character flaw—it's how your brain works:

  • Hyperfocus states feel amazing but are neurologically expensive
  • Crashes aren't laziness—they're your brain recovering from intense activity
  • The cycle is predictable once you start tracking it

🚀 During High-Energy Periods

Don't Go 100% Full Throttle:

  • Pace yourself to 70-80% of your maximum energy
  • Set timers to take breaks even when you don't want to
  • Leave breadcrumbs for your future self (notes about what you were doing, where you left off)
  • Save some energy for basic life maintenance (eating, sleeping, hygiene)

Prepare for the Crash:

  • Meal prep or stock up on easy foods
  • Do laundry and basic chores while you have energy
  • Set up your recovery space (comfort items, entertainment, etc.)
  • Clear your schedule for the days after big creative pushes

📉 During Low-Energy Periods

Reframe the Crash:

  • This is recovery, not failure
  • Your brain is processing and consolidating all that creative work
  • Low periods are part of the cycle, not a sign something's wrong

Gentle Maintenance:

  • Lower your standards temporarily—survival mode is okay
  • Use "minimum viable" versions of self-care (shower wipes vs. full shower)
  • Have easy entertainment ready (comfort shows, audiobooks, etc.)
  • Don't judge yourself for needing recovery time

🎯 Long-Term Strategies

Track Your Patterns:

  • Keep a simple energy/mood log to identify your cycles
  • Notice triggers that start creative periods
  • Track how long highs and lows typically last
  • Identify early warning signs of crashes

Build Sustainable Systems:

  • Create consistent routines that work regardless of energy level
  • Build in recovery time after big projects
  • Set up support systems (friends who understand, automatic bill pay, etc.)
  • Practice saying no to additional commitments during recovery periods

🧰 Emergency Crash Kit:

  • Comfort items: weighted blanket, favorite stuffed animal, soft clothes
  • Easy food: granola bars, instant meals, favorite snacks
  • Gentle entertainment: comfort shows, familiar music, easy books
  • Self-compassion reminders: notes to yourself about why this is normal
  • Basic necessities: medications, hygiene items, phone charger

Remember: The goal isn't to eliminate the cycles—it's to manage them sustainably so the crashes are less severe and you can enjoy the creative periods without burning out completely.

7. Pacing During High Energy Periods

Question: but it's hard to pace myself when I'm in the flow state. I feel like I'm wasting the opportunity if I don't go all out

I totally get this! This is one of the hardest parts about managing creative cycles. That flow state feels so good and productive that stopping feels like betrayal. But here's the thing:

🎯 Reframing "Wasting the Opportunity"

Going all-out isn't maximizing the opportunity—it's borrowing from your future self.

Think of it this way:

  • Scenario A: You go 100% for 3 days, then crash for 2 weeks
  • Scenario B: You go 70% for 5 days, then need 3 days to recover

Which scenario actually gives you more productive time over a month?

🔄 The Sustainability Mindset

Flow States Will Come Again:

  • Flow isn't a rare unicorn—it's a recurring state for you
  • By pacing yourself, you'll have more flow states over time
  • Quality work comes from sustainable practices, not heroic efforts

Your Best Work Needs Processing Time:

  • Great ideas need time to marinate
  • Stepping back gives you perspective on what you've created
  • Rest periods are when your brain makes connections

🛠️ Practical Pacing Strategies

The "90/10 Rule":

  • 90% of your time in flow should be spent on the creative work
  • 10% should be spent preparing for the inevitable end of the flow state
  • This isn't "wasting" the flow—it's maximizing its long-term value

Micro-Breaks That Don't Kill Flow:

  • Hydration breaks (30 seconds to chug water)
  • Bathroom breaks (don't ignore your body)
  • Quick stretches (reach for the ceiling, roll shoulders)
  • Deep breathing (3 breaths while thinking about your next step)

Flow State Documentation:

  • Keep a running log of what you're working on
  • Voice record your thoughts as you work
  • Take photos of your workspace/progress
  • Write quick notes about what to do next

🎨 Protecting Future Creative Sessions

Leave Breadcrumbs:

  • "Tomorrow, I want to..." notes
  • Save interesting problems for when you return
  • Set up materials for easy re-entry
  • Document your process so you can recreate the conditions

The "Just One More" Trap:

  • Set a timer and honor it (even if it feels arbitrary)
  • Tell yourself "This idea will be here tomorrow"
  • Remember that forcing it often leads to diminishing returns
  • Practice ending while you still have energy

🧠 Mental Reframes for Flow State Guilt:

  • "I'm investing in my next flow state"
  • "Sustainable creativity is more valuable than burst creativity"
  • "My brain needs time to process this work"
  • "I can't pour from an empty cup"
  • "Rest is part of the creative process"

The paradox: By "wasting" the opportunity to go all-out, you're actually creating more opportunities for flow states. It's playing the long game instead of the short game.

8. Creation vs Consumption Balance

Question: I've noticed that when I'm in the down periods I tend to just consume content (youtube, social media, etc.) but that seems to make the depression worse. Is there a connection?

Absolutely! You've identified a really important pattern. There's a strong connection between passive consumption and depression, especially during recovery periods.

🧠 The Consumption-Depression Loop

Why Passive Consumption Worsens Depression:

  • Lack of agency: You're not creating or influencing anything, just receiving
  • Comparison trap: Social media especially triggers "everyone else is doing better" thoughts
  • Dopamine dysregulation: Constant micro-hits of dopamine from scrolling can mess with your reward system
  • No sense of accomplishment: You're not building anything or moving toward goals
  • Information overload: Your already-tired brain gets more stimulation when it needs rest

The Autistic Brain Factor:

  • Overstimulation: Your brain is already processing recovery, and content consumption adds more to process
  • Social comparison: Seeing neurotypical people's highlight reels can trigger masking shame
  • Executive function drain: Endless scrolling requires constant micro-decisions that exhaust you further

🔄 The Creation-Consumption Balance

Theory: Humans need both creation and consumption, but depression often tips the balance too far toward consumption.

What "Creation" Means During Low Periods:

It doesn't have to be big! Even tiny creative acts can help:

  • Writing one sentence in a journal
  • Doodling or coloring
  • Organizing your phone photos
  • Making a playlist
  • Texting a friend about your day
  • Taking a photo of something interesting
  • Moving furniture around

🛠️ Practical Strategies

The "20% Rule":

  • Aim for 20% creation, 80% consumption during low periods
  • This prevents the complete creative shutdown that deepens depression
  • It's not about productivity—it's about maintaining agency and self-expression

Mindful Consumption:

  • Choose content intentionally rather than falling into algorithm rabbitholes
  • Watch/read things that inspire or comfort rather than stress
  • Time-box consumption (e.g., "I'll watch YouTube for 30 minutes")
  • Avoid comparison-heavy platforms during vulnerable periods

Low-Energy Creation Ideas:

  • Voice memos to your future self
  • Take photos of your mood or surroundings
  • Write stream-of-consciousness for 5 minutes
  • Make something with your hands (LEGO, clay, etc.)
  • Organize something (files, bookmarks, a drawer)
  • Edit or curate existing content

🎯 The Bridge Back to High Energy

Creation as Recovery:

  • Small creative acts can be bridges back to flow states
  • They remind you that you're capable of making things
  • They provide evidence of your agency in the world
  • They often spark ideas for bigger projects

🧰 Low-Energy Creation Toolkit:

  • Journal prompts written on index cards
  • Art supplies that require minimal setup
  • Voice recording app for quick thoughts
  • Camera for documenting your world
  • Simple instruments (ukulele, kalimba, etc.)
  • Building toys (LEGO, magnetic tiles)
  • Organizational projects you can do from bed

Key insight: The goal isn't to be productive during low periods—it's to maintain a tiny spark of creativity that keeps you connected to your identity as a creator, not just a consumer.

10. The Dopamine Connection: Coding vs MDMA

Yes — that's a very apt comparison. What you're describing is strikingly similar to the dopamine crash people experience after using drugs like MDMA (ecstasy).

Let's break this down — you're on point.

🧪 Dopamine Highs: Coding vs. MDMA

Mechanism Coding Flow MDMA (Ecstasy)
Trigger Deep focus, creative obsession, emotional engagement Chemical flood of serotonin & dopamine
Effect Euphoria, clarity, connection to purpose Euphoria, empathy, emotional openness
Duration Hours or days of obsession 4–6 hours typically
Aftermath Emotional emptiness, disinterest, self-doubt "Tuesday blues," depression, low motivation
Cause of Crash Dopamine depletion & mental exhaustion Neurotransmitter depletion, receptor downregulation

🔬 What's Actually Happening?

During the High:

  • Your brain floods itself with dopamine as you achieve flow, solve problems, or pursue meaning.
  • This feels amazing. You are "on" — creative, connected, unstoppable.

After the High:

  • Your brain's dopamine system needs time to reset.
  • Until it does, you feel flat, aimless, or depressed — not because something went wrong, but because you used up a finite resource.

This is very similar to the post-MDMA comedown — only your drug is your passion and the dose is focus.

🧠 Why This Is So Common in Autistic and ADHD Brains

  • We tend to have dopamine regulation differences — underactive in baseline states, hyperactive during focus or stimulation.
  • That means when we finally do feel flow or excitement, we go all-in… and often overdo it.
  • Then we crash, not just emotionally but neurologically.

This is sometimes referred to as dopaminergic dysregulation — and it's central to both autistic burnout and ADHD motivation crashes.

🛡️ What To Do About It

Just like with MDMA, you can't (and shouldn't) try to stay high forever.

But you can:

  • Pace the release (limit session time, insert breaks)
  • Support recovery (sleep, protein, sunlight, movement)
  • Avoid shame spirals during the crash
  • Use scaffolding like notes, rituals, and gentle reentry tasks

Think of it like harm reduction for your own creative chemistry.

🧘‍♂️ Reframe It

Instead of fearing the crash, start respecting the arc:

"This high is a gift — and when it fades, I'll rest. I'll leave a note for the next rise."

That mindset turns a destructive cycle into a sustainable rhythm.

  • Recognize the pattern - Your creative highs are neurochemical events
  • Respect the crash - It's not weakness, it's biology
  • Support recovery - Sleep, nutrition, gentle movement
  • Practice harm reduction - Limit session intensity and duration
  • Leave breadcrumbs - Document your high states for future reference
